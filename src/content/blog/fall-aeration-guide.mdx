Every September we aerate hundreds of lawns in a six-week sprint, and every spring the difference between treated and untreated lawns is obvious from the street. Here is why fall aeration earns its reputation.

## What Aeration Actually Does

A core aerator pulls thousands of finger-sized soil plugs from the lawn. Each hole is a channel for air, water, and fertilizer to reach the root zone directly, and each one relieves the compaction that builds up from a season of mowing, foot traffic, and rain. On the heavy clay soils common around Springfield, this matters more than almost anything else you can do.

## Why Fall Beats Spring

- **Warm soil, cool air.** Seed germinates fast in 60–70°F soil while cool nights keep seedlings from cooking.
- **No crabgrass competition.** Annual weeds are dying off in September, so new grass gets the space to itself. Spring seeding fights weeds for every inch.
- **Two growing windows.** Fall seedlings get autumn plus the following spring to mature before facing their first summer.

## How to Water New Seed

This is where results are won or lost:

1. **Weeks 1–3:** Keep the top half-inch of soil consistently moist — light watering once or twice daily.
2. **Weeks 4–6:** Water deeper, every other day, to pull roots downward.
3. **After establishment:** Return to the normal one inch per week.

## Leave the Plugs Alone

The soil cores on the surface look messy for two or three weeks, then rain and mowing break them down into a natural topdressing. Raking them up throws away free soil amendment.
