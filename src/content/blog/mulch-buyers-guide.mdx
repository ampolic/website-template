Mulch is the cheapest landscaping upgrade with the biggest visual payoff — but half the mulch we see installed around Springfield is doing more harm than good. Here's how to get it right.

## What Mulch Is Actually For

Looks come first for most homeowners, but the real work happens underneath: a proper mulch layer holds soil moisture through summer, insulates roots from temperature swings, blocks the light weed seeds need to germinate, and slowly feeds the soil as it breaks down.

## Choosing a Type

- **Natural cedar** — light golden color, naturally insect-resistant, breaks down slowly. Ages to gray by late season.
- **Dyed hardwood (brown or black)** — holds its color the entire season and costs less. Black reads crisp against green turf; brown is the safe default.
- **Playground-certified wood fiber** — the right choice under swing sets and in dog runs.
- **Stone** — permanent and tidy but bakes root zones in summer heat. Best for drainage strips, not planting beds.

## How Much You Need

Two to three inches of depth, no more. One cubic yard covers roughly 100 square feet at 3 inches. Measure your beds, multiply length by width, and divide — or have us measure and quote it by the yard.

## The Mulch Volcano Problem

The cone of mulch piled against a tree trunk — the "mulch volcano" — is slowly killing trees all over town. Bark buried in wet mulch rots, girdling roots circle the trunk, and rodents nest against the bark all winter. Keep mulch pulled back three inches from every trunk and stem, in a flat donut, never a cone. Your trees will outlive the mistake-makers' by decades.
