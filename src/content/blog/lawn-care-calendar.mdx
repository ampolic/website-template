Most lawn problems we get called about in July were actually decided in March. Here is the calendar we run our own programs on, adapted for homeowners doing some or all of the work themselves.

## Spring (March – May)

- **March:** Clean up leaves and debris as soon as the lawn is dry enough to walk on without leaving footprints. Inspect irrigation for winter damage.
- **April:** Apply crabgrass pre-emergent before soil temperatures hit 55°F — in central Illinois that's usually when the forsythia blooms. First mow at 3 inches.
- **May:** Begin weekly mowing. Spot-treat broadleaf weeds while they're young. Prune spring-flowering shrubs right after they finish blooming.

## Summer (June – August)

- Raise your mowing height to 3.5–4 inches — taller grass shades the soil and needs less water.
- Water deeply and infrequently: about one inch per week, delivered in one or two sessions, early morning.
- Watch for grubs in late July; brown patches that lift like carpet are the tell.
- Skip fertilizing in extreme heat. A stressed lawn can't use it.

## Fall (September – November)

- **September:** The single best month for your lawn. Aerate, overseed, and feed — cool nights and warm soil make seed germination almost effortless.
- **October:** Keep mowing as long as the grass grows. Mulch leaves into the lawn until they get too thick, then remove them.
- **November:** Final mow at 2.5 inches, apply winterizer fertilizer, and blow out irrigation before the first hard freeze.

## Winter (December – February)

- Stay off frozen turf where you can — foot traffic on frost breaks grass blades.
- Sharpen mower blades and service equipment now, not in April when every shop has a three-week backlog.
- Plan next season: this is the best time to book spring cleanups and mulch work before schedules fill.
